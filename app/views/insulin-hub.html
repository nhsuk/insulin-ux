Insulin for diabetes

Insulin is a hormone that helps your body use glucose (sugar) for energy.

It’s used as a medicine to treat diabetes, including type 1 diabetes, type 2 diabetes and gestational diabetes. It’s available on prescription.

Most people with type 1 diabetes take a long acting background insulin once or twice a day, and a separate rapid acting insulin before food. This is sometimes known as a basal bolus regime.

You’ll usually be offered insulin detemir (Levemir) twice a day as background insulin.

Some people take a longer acting background insulin called insulin glargine (Lantus) or insulin degludec (Tresiba) once a day. You might be advised to take insulin degludec if you get hypos at night.

You’ll usually also be offered a rapid acting insulin called either insulin aspart (NovoRapid or Fiasp), insulin lispro (Humalog) or insulin glulisine (Apidra) to take before meals.

You’ll need to calculate your dose for each meal, depending on the amount of carbohydrates you’ll be eating (carb counting). Carb counting and adjusting your insulin dose gives you flexibility in what you can eat.

If you use an insulin pump, you’ll only take a rapid acting insulin. Having small doses throughout the day means you do not need a separate background insulin.
